# Window Orientation Matters More Than Window Type

## The Counterintuitive Truth

Homeowners spend thousands upgrading from double-pane to triple-pane windows. Meanwhile, they have 80 square feet of glass facing due west, baking their living room every summer afternoon.

**The orientation of your windows has a larger impact on your cooling load than the window type itself.**

## Solar Heat Gain by Direction

The sun's intensity on a vertical surface varies dramatically by compass direction. For a typical summer day at 35°N latitude:

| Direction | Peak Solar BTU/hr per sqft | Relative Impact |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **West** | 190-220 BTU | 🔴 Maximum — afternoon sun at low angle hits glass directly |
| **East** | 180-210 BTU | 🟡 High — morning sun, same low-angle problem |
| **South** | 80-120 BTU | 🟢 Moderate — high sun angle means less direct penetration |
| **North** | 20-40 BTU | 🔵 Minimal — diffuse skylight only, no direct beam |

A west-facing window delivers **5-10× more solar heat** than a north-facing window of the same size and type.

## The Math That Changes Equipment Sizing

Consider two identical 2,000 sqft homes in Phoenix:

**Home A**: 80 sqft of windows facing south and north (40 each)
- Solar gain: (40 × 100) + (40 × 30) = **5,200 BTU/hr**

**Home B**: 80 sqft of windows facing east and west (40 each)  
- Solar gain: (40 × 200) + (40 × 200) = **16,000 BTU/hr**

Same total window area. Same window type. **Home B needs nearly a full extra ton of cooling** just from window orientation.

## What This Means for Design

### New Construction
- Maximize south-facing glass (easy to shade with roof overhangs due to high summer sun angle)
- Minimize west-facing glass (hardest to shade — low afternoon sun goes under overhangs)
- North-facing glass is essentially "free" in cooling-dominated climates

### Existing Homes
- **Prioritize shading on west-facing windows** — exterior shades, awnings, or solar screens reduce gain by 60-80%
- Upgrading west-facing windows to low-SHGC glass has the highest ROI
- Interior blinds help, but only reduce solar gain by 30-40% (the heat is already inside)

## Don't Forget Winter

In heating-dominated climates, south-facing windows are extremely beneficial. They capture free solar heat during winter when the sun is low in the sky, potentially offsetting thousands of BTU per hour in heating load.

This is the fundamental principle behind passive solar design: maximize south glass, minimize north glass, and let the sun do the heating work for free.
