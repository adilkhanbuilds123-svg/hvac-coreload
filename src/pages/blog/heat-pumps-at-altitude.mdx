# Heat Pumps at Altitude: Why Denver Needs Different Math

## The Altitude Problem Nobody Talks About

Every piece of HVAC equipment in America is rated at sea level. The nameplate that says "36,000 BTU" was tested with air at standard density: 0.075 lb/ft³.

But in Denver at 5,280 feet, air density drops to **0.062 lb/ft³** — a 17% reduction. In Santa Fe at 7,000 feet, it's 23% less dense. In Leadville at 10,152 feet, it's 31% thinner.

**Your 3-ton heat pump does not deliver 3 tons at altitude.**

## The Physics

The sensible cooling capacity of any air-side system is:

```
Q = 1.08 × CFM × ΔT × (ρ/ρ₀)
```

Where `ρ/ρ₀` is the ratio of local air density to sea-level density. At 5,280 feet:

```
Q = 1.08 × CFM × ΔT × 0.83
```

That 3-ton system (36,000 BTU rated) actually delivers approximately **29,880 BTU** in Denver. You lost half a ton of capacity to thin air.

## Impact on Equipment Selection

This means Manual S equipment selection at altitude requires **de-rating every piece of equipment** by the altitude correction factor. A home with a 30,000 BTU cooling load in Denver needs equipment that is rated for:

```
30,000 / 0.83 = 36,145 BTU at sea level rating
```

You need a 3-ton rated unit to deliver 2.5 tons of actual capacity.

## The Heating Side Is Worse

Heat pumps extract thermal energy from outdoor air. At altitude, there's less air mass moving across the outdoor coil per unit time. Combined with Denver's design temperature of 1°F, heat pump capacity at the balance point can be shockingly low.

A heat pump rated at 40,000 BTU heating at 47°F (the AHRI rating point) might deliver only **18,000 BTU** at 1°F and 5,280 feet. The altitude correction compounds with the cold-temperature capacity curve.

## What Contractors Get Wrong

1. **Ignoring altitude entirely** — Using sea-level nameplate capacity for equipment selection
2. **Using the rule of thumb** — "One ton per 500 sqft" is already wrong at sea level; at altitude it's doubly wrong
3. **Undersizing auxiliary heat** — Not accounting for the gap between heat pump capacity and heating load at the balance point
4. **Wrong duct sizing** — Lower air density means you need larger ducts or higher fan speeds to move the same mass of air
